Phalguna · February–March

Sri Gaura Purnima

श्री गौर पूर्णिमा — गौराङ्ग महाप्रभु का आविर्भाव दिवस

The most sacred celebration in the Gaudiya Vaishnava calendar

The Story Behind the Festival

On the full-moon night of the month of Phalguna in 1486 CE, in the town of Nabadwip (present-day Mayapur, West Bengal), Sri Chaitanya Mahaprabhu appeared in this world. Born as Vishwambhar to Jagannath Mishra and Sachi Devi, He was the golden-complexioned avatar prophesied in the Vedic scriptures — the Supreme Lord appearing in the mood of His own devotee to teach the path of divine love.

At His birth, a lunar eclipse was in progress. Hindus across Bengal were gathered at the Ganges, chanting the holy names as was customary during an eclipse. The entire atmosphere resounded with "Hare Krishna, Hare Rama" — and into this ocean of the holy name, the Lord chose to descend. This is why Gaura Purnima is celebrated on the full moon that coincides (or recalls) this divine event.

"I shall accept the sentiment and complexion of Sri Radha and descend to understand three things: the glory of Her love, the wonder of the love She experiences when She realises My sweetness, and the joy She experiences."

Common Practices & Traditions

  • Fasting until moonrise — Devotees observe a complete fast (nirjala) or a simple fast throughout the day, breaking it only after the moon rises in the evening.
  • Sankirtan & Kirtan — Extended sessions of congregational chanting. The songs of Narottam Das Thakur and other Vaishnava poets are sung with great devotion.
  • Abhishek ceremony — The deities are bathed with panchamrit (milk, yoghurt, honey, ghee, and sugar water), decorated with fresh flowers and new garments.
  • Reading the Chaitanya Charitamrita — Passages describing the Lord's appearance, childhood pastimes, and the inauguration of the sankirtan movement are recited.
  • Grand prasadam feast — After moonrise, a sumptuous vegetarian feast is offered to the Lord and distributed to all devotees.

How We Celebrate at Math Mandir Chandigarh

At the Sri Chaitanya Gaudiya Math in Sector 20, Gaura Purnima is the spiritual high point of the year. The celebrations typically span two to three days, with the Math beautifully decorated with marigolds, mango leaves, and colourful rangoli.

The morning begins with a special Mangala Arati, followed by hours of non-stop kirtan. Senior devotees deliver harikatha on the glories of Mahaprabhu — His sankirtan movement, His teachings in the Siksastakam, and the revolutionary message of unconditional love He brought to the world.

In the evening, as the full moon rises over Chandigarh, the main abhishek of the deities takes place amidst thunderous chanting. The atmosphere becomes electric with devotion. Following the abhishek, a magnificent feast is served to hundreds of devotees and visitors from across the tri-city area.

Significance in the Gaudiya Tradition

For Gaudiya Vaishnavas, Gaura Purnima holds a status comparable to — and in some ways surpassing — that of Janmashtami. Sri Chaitanya Mahaprabhu is understood not merely as a saint or reformer, but as the Supreme Lord Himself, appearing in the most merciful form to freely distribute the treasure of prema (divine love) through the simple, universal practice of chanting the Hare Krishna Mahamantra.

His mission was to establish that in this age (Kali Yuga), there is no other way, no other way, no other way of spiritual attainment except the congregational chanting of the holy name — harer nāma harer nāma harer nāmaiva kevalam.
