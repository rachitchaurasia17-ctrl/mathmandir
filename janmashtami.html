Bhadra · August–September

Sri Krishna Janmashtami

श्री कृष्ण जन्माष्टमी — भगवान श्रीकृष्ण का आविर्भाव दिवस

The midnight appearance of the Supreme Lord Sri Krishna

The Story Behind the Festival

Over five thousand years ago, the Earth was burdened by the sinful activities of tyrannical rulers. The devatas (demigods) prayed to Lord Vishnu for deliverance. The Supreme Lord promised to appear on Earth to establish dharma and to display His transcendental pastimes of love.

On the eighth day (Ashtami) of the waning moon in the month of Bhadra, at the stroke of midnight, Lord Krishna appeared in the prison cell of Mathura to Vasudeva and Devaki. Despite the heavy rain, lightning, and Kamsa's guards, Vasudeva miraculously carried baby Krishna across the flooding Yamuna to Gokul, where He was raised by His foster parents Nanda Maharaj and Yashoda Maiya.

Krishna's appearance was not ordinary birth — it was the descent (avatara) of the Supreme Personality of Godhead, complete with His eternal form, ornaments, and divine weapons, instantly appearing before His parents in His four-armed Vishnu form before assuming the form of a newborn baby.

"Whenever and wherever there is a decline in religious practice and a predominant rise of irreligion, at that time I descend Myself." — Bhagavad Gita 4.7

Common Practices & Traditions

  • Nirjala fasting — Devotees observe a strict waterless fast throughout the day, breaking it only after midnight when the Lord's appearance is celebrated.
  • Midnight Abhishek — At exactly midnight (the time of Krishna's appearance), the deity of baby Krishna is bathed with panchamrit in a grand ceremony accompanied by ecstatic kirtan.
  • Dahi Handi — In many communities, a pot of curd is hung high and young men form human pyramids to break it, re-enacting Krishna's childhood pastime of stealing butter.
  • Jhulanotsav (Swing Festival) — The deity of baby Krishna is placed on a beautifully decorated swing, and devotees gently rock the swing while singing lullabies.
  • Srimad Bhagavatam Recital — The 10th Canto, which describes Krishna's childhood pastimes in Vrindavan, is read and narrated throughout the day.
  • 56 Bhog (Chhappan Bhog) — An elaborate offering of 56 food items is prepared for the Lord, later distributed as mahaprasadam.

How We Celebrate at Math Mandir Chandigarh

Janmashtami at the Chandigarh Math is a night to remember. The temple is transformed with elaborate floral decorations, and a special jhanki (tableau) depicting the Mathura prison scene is created with meticulous detail.

The day begins with extended Bhagavatam recitals narrating Krishna's appearance lila. As evening approaches, the kirtan intensifies — hours of continuous chanting build to a crescendo at midnight. The Math invites renowned kirtaniyas and speakers from across India for this occasion.

At the stroke of midnight, the conch shells blow, the curtains of the altar part to reveal the magnificently decorated deities, and the abhishek ceremony begins. The entire congregation erupts in jubilant kirtan. Following the ceremony, a feast of over 56 preparations is served to all — often to crowds of several hundred who gather from across the tri-city area.

Spiritual Significance

Janmashtami is not merely a historical commemoration — it is a celebration of the eternal truth that God descends to this world out of His causeless mercy. For Gaudiya Vaishnavas, Krishna is not an avatar of Vishnu, but the original Supreme Person (krishnas tu bhagavan svayam) from whom all other forms emanate.

The teachings He delivered in the Bhagavad Gita and demonstrated through His Vrindavan pastimes form the very foundation of devotional life. Every Janmashtami is an invitation to deepen our surrender and remember that the Lord is always accessible through love, service, and the chanting of His holy names.
