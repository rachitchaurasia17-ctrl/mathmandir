Ashadha · June–July

Sri Jagannath Rath Yatra

श्री जगन्नाथ रथ यात्रा — महाप्रभु का पावन रथोत्सव

The grand chariot festival of love and devotion

The Story Behind the Festival

The Rath Yatra (Chariot Festival) originated in Puri, Odisha, and commemorates Lord Jagannath's annual visit to the Gundicha Temple, representing His journey back to His childhood home in Vrindavan. Lord Jagannath (Krishna), along with His brother Balabhadra and sister Subhadra, ride on massive, elaborately decorated wooden chariots.

The profound inner meaning of the festival, as revealed by Sri Chaitanya Mahaprabhu, is the intense longing and love of the gopis (the cowherd girls of Vrindavan) wanting to pull Krishna back from the opulent city of Dwarka to the simple, sweet village of Vrindavan.

Mahaprabhu Himself would dance in divine ecstasy before Lord Jagannath's chariot during the festival in Puri, fully absorbed in the mood of Srimati Radharani's supreme devotion.

"As Lord Jagannath rode upon His chariot, Sri Chaitanya Mahaprabhu danced in front of Him. The Lord's dancing was so beautiful that all the people present were struck with wonder." — Chaitanya Charitamrita

Common Practices & Traditions

  • Pulling the Chariots — Devotees eagerly pull the thick ropes of the chariots, considering it a great blessing that burns away lifetimes of sinful reactions.
  • Sankirtan — Huge groups of devotees surround the chariots, loudly chanting the Hare Krishna Maha Mantra and playing mridangas and kartals.
  • Chhera Panhara — The king (or head of the community) sweeps the road before the chariots with a golden broom, demonstrating that before the Lord, even a king is a humble servant.
  • Gundicha Marjan — The day before the Rath Yatra, the destination temple (Gundicha) is thoroughly washed and cleaned by the devotees, symbolizing the cleansing of one's own heart to receive the Lord.

How We Celebrate at Math Mandir Chandigarh

The Chandigarh Math organizes a magnificent and joyous Rath Yatra festival that takes to the streets of the city. A beautifully crafted chariot carrying the deities of Lord Jagannath, Lord Balabhadra, and Srimati Subhadra is pulled by hundreds of enthusiastic devotees.

The procession is accompanied by continuous, ecstatic kirtan that reverberates through the streets. Prasadam is distributed generously to all onlookers and participants along the route, turning the event into a massive spiritual feast for the whole city.

Prior to the festival, the Math also observes Snana Yatra (the bathing festival of the deities) and the period of anavasara, when the deities "fall ill" and are offered special herbal preparations in seclusion before emerging for the grand Rath Yatra.

Spiritual Significance

Rath Yatra is unique because it is the one time of the year when the Lord comes out of the temple to give darshan to all, without discrimination. It is the festival of the Lord's supreme compassion, reaching out to those who may never visit a temple.

Spiritually, pulling the chariot is a meditation on pulling the Lord back into the temple of one's own heart. By participating in this festival and chanting the holy names, one invites Lord Jagannath to reside eternally within their consciousness.
